--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 1 — PROBLEM STATEMENT AND MOTIVATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Modern compute clusters, particularly those serving academic and research
workloads, exhibit sharp, unpredictable spikes in CPU and GPU utilisation.
These spikes cause sustained thermal elevation that, if unmanaged, leads to
thermal throttling, hardware degradation, and silent performance loss.

Reactive systems — those that act only after a threshold is already crossed —
are fundamentally too slow. By the time a VM reports CPU temperature of 82°C,
the thermal damage window has already opened. The cooling lag in real hardware
(thermal inertia modelled as alpha = 0.15 in this system) means temperature
continues rising for several minutes even after load is removed.

TADR addresses this with a predictive control loop: predict where temperature
will be 30 minutes from now, measure how fast it is approaching danger, and
act before the threshold is hit — redistributing tasks if capacity exists,
scaling out if the entire fleet is converging on danger simultaneously.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 2 — DATASET: WHAT IT IS AND WHY IT IS STRUCTURED THIS WAY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The training dataset covers March 1 to December 31, 2021 at 1-minute intervals,
producing 440,640 rows. This is not arbitrary — 1-minute granularity is the
finest resolution at which meaningful thermal trends manifest. Sub-minute
intervals would add noise without predictive value; coarser intervals (5-minute)
would smooth out the rapid onset bursts that the system is specifically designed
to catch.

Each row contains 15 columns across four groups:

  CLIMATIC FACTORS
    Ambient Temperature (°C)     — Bangalore seasonal pattern: 20°C Dec,
                                   29°C April peak, 24°C monsoon months.
                                   Diurnal swing modelled as sinusoidal with
                                   monthly amplitude variation (4–7°C range).
    Humidity (%)                 — Inversely correlated with ambient temp.
                                   Monsoon months (Jun–Sep): 72–82%.
                                   Dry season (Mar–May, Dec): 38–48%.
    AQI (Air Quality Index)      — Traffic-correlated peaks at 08–10h and
                                   17–20h. Monsoon rain suppresses particulates.
                                   Range: 45–120.

  ELECTRICAL PARAMETERS
    Voltage (V), Current (A), Power (W), Frequency (Hz),
    Active Energy (kWh), Power Factor
    These reflect real power draw behaviour: voltage sags slightly under
    heavy load, current rises with combined CPU+GPU load, power factor
    drops marginally at high load. Active Energy resets per day with a
    realistic 81.9 kWh base offset.

  COMPUTE METRICS
    CPU consumption (%)          — Load follows dual-peak diurnal pattern.
                                   Academic spike days push this to 99%.
    CPU Temperature (°C)         — Physics-based: base 42°C + load×0.36
                                   + nonlinear term (load/100)²×12 +
                                   ambient contribution. Crosses 80°C on
                                   academic exam and submission days.
    GPU consumption (%)          — Correlated with CPU (×0.72) with
                                   independent burst events for rendering
                                   and simulation jobs.
    GPU Temperature (°C)         — Higher baseline (48°C idle), steeper
                                   load curve (load×0.38). Designed to enter
                                   the 80–85°C band under sustained load.

  CALENDAR STRUCTURE
    The dataset embeds five academic event periods (mid-sem exams Apr 19–28,
    end-sem Jun 7–18, internals Aug 16–20, mid-sem Oct 11–20, end-sem+
    projects Dec 1–15) and six vacation blocks where load drops to 25% of
    normal. This gives the model real exposure to the full dynamic range:
    idle/vacation behaviour, normal operation, and sustained spike events —
    all essential for a model that needs to generalise across operational
    contexts, not just average conditions.

The time-ordered 85/15 train-test split (shuffle=False) is deliberate.
Shuffling would allow the model to see "future" rows during training, producing
optimistic evaluation metrics that collapse in production. Time-ordered splitting
forces the model to be evaluated strictly on the chronological tail of the data —
the closest analogue to real deployment conditions.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 3 — FEATURE ENGINEERING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Ten features are constructed for each training sample:

  1.  CPU consumption (%)
        Direct predictor of heat generation. The single strongest feature
        because thermal output scales with computational load.

  2.  GPU consumption (%)
        Independent heat source. GPU-heavy workloads (ML training, video
        rendering) raise chassis temperature independently of CPU load.

  3.  CPU Temperature (°C)  — current
        The most informative single feature for predicting near-term
        temperature. The current value anchors the prediction.

  4.  GPU Temperature (°C)  — current
        Captures GPU thermal state independently. In GPU-dominated workloads
        this becomes the primary heat source.

  5.  prev_cpu_temp
        Temperature at the immediately prior minute. Together with (3),
        this gives the model the first derivative of temperature: whether
        the system is heating or cooling at the moment of prediction.

  6.  prev_gpu_temp
        Same purpose for GPU thermal trajectory.

  7.  delta_cpu_temp  =  CPU_temp[t] − CPU_temp[t−1]
        Explicit first-order difference. Positive delta signals active
        heating; negative signals cooling trend. This is redundant with
        (3) and (5) algebraically, but RandomForest decision trees do not
        compute differences internally — providing it explicitly improves
        splits at boundary conditions.

  8.  delta_gpu_temp
        Same for GPU.

  9.  hour_sin  =  sin(2π × hour / 24)
  10. hour_cos  =  cos(2π × hour / 24)
        Cyclic encoding of the time of day. A single raw "hour" integer
        treats 23 and 0 as distant (difference = 23) when they are actually
        adjacent (difference = 1). Sine-cosine encoding maps every hour to
        a point on the unit circle, preserving cyclical continuity. This
        lets the model learn that midnight behaviour is similar to 11 PM,
        and that peak load at 10h and 15h should elicit similar thermal
        responses — without needing separate training examples for every
        hour combination.

The target for both models is temperature HORIZON minutes (30 rows) ahead,
constructed via pandas shift(-30). This makes every training sample a
supervised regression problem: given the current state of a VM, predict
where its temperature will be in 30 minutes.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4 — MODEL ARCHITECTURE: RANDOMFORESTREGRESSOR
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Two separate models are trained: one for CPU temperature, one for GPU
temperature. They share the same architecture and feature space but are
trained against independent targets.

  Architecture:
    RandomForestRegressor(
        n_estimators   = 100,
        max_depth      = 12,
        min_samples_leaf = 5,
        n_jobs         = -1,
        random_state   = 42
    )

WHY RANDOMFOREST:

  1. Non-parametric. It makes no assumption about the functional form of
     the temperature-load relationship. The actual relationship is nonlinear
     (thermal physics follows exponential approach to steady-state, not linear
     growth), and RandomForest captures this without explicit polynomial
     feature expansion.

  2. Ensemble variance is meaningful. The 100 trees each see a bootstrap
     sample of the data and a random subset of features. Their predictions
     disagree in proportion to how ambiguous the input region is. This
     disagreement is exploited directly to compute confidence (see Section 5).
     A single regression tree or a linear model cannot produce this signal.

  3. No normalisation required. Decision tree splits are threshold-based,
     not distance-based. Features do not need to be on the same scale.
     CPU% (0–100) and hour_sin (−1 to +1) can coexist without distorting
     the model.

  4. Robust to the irregular spike structure in the data. During exam
     periods, load patterns deviate sharply from baseline. The bagging in
     RandomForest (each tree trained on a bootstrap resample) naturally
     downweights the influence of any single spike event, producing models
     that generalise rather than memorise individual bursts.

  max_depth = 12 is a deliberate regularisation choice. Deeper trees would
  perfectly fit the training set but would be extremely sensitive to the
  exact trajectory of each individual spike. At depth 12, trees capture the
  dominant patterns (load → temp, diurnal cycle, thermal inertia) without
  overfitting to the specific academic calendar of 2021.

  min_samples_leaf = 5 ensures that no leaf node is based on fewer than 5
  training examples. This prevents the tree from making confident predictions
  about rare spike configurations seen only once or twice in training.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 5 — INFERENCE: CONFIDENCE AND VELOCITY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

At runtime, every Kafka tick causes predictor.predict(vm) to be called for
every registered VM. Here is exactly what happens:

STEP 1 — Feature construction

  _build_features(vm) assembles a (1 × 10) numpy array:
    [cpu, gpu, temp, prev_temp, delta_temp, hour_sin, hour_cos]
  Note: at inference time only 7 features are available (not 10), because
  prev_gpu and delta_gpu require historical GPU temperature which the live
  metric stream may not always carry. The training feature set uses 10 columns;
  the inference vector must match exactly what train_models.py built.
  This is a known implementation coupling — the feature column list in
  train_models.py and _build_features() must be kept in sync.

STEP 2 — Ensemble prediction

  _ensemble_predict(model, X) iterates over model.estimators_ (the list of
  100 trained decision trees) and collects each tree's prediction:

    tree_preds = [tree.predict(X)[0] for tree in model.estimators_]
    μ = mean(tree_preds)
    σ = std(tree_preds)

  This produces the ensemble mean prediction and the ensemble standard
  deviation. The mean is the actual temperature forecast. The standard
  deviation is the uncertainty estimate.

  Why does σ measure uncertainty? Each tree was trained on a different
  bootstrap sample of the training data and makes splits on a different
  random subset of features. When trees agree (small σ), it means that
  regardless of which subset of data or features is used, the predicted
  temperature is similar — the mapping is robust and consistent. When trees
  disagree (large σ), it means the input region is one where different
  subsets of training data point to different conclusions — the prediction
  is inherently less reliable.

  This is the connection to probabilistic forecasting. If we treat the
  ensemble as approximating p(y | x) ~ N(μ, σ²), then:
    — High σ means a wider posterior distribution over future temperature
    — Low σ means the posterior is tight around μ

STEP 3 — Confidence mapping

  confidence = 1 / (1 + σ)

  This is a heuristic inverse-uncertainty mapping. It maps σ ∈ [0, ∞)
  monotonically to confidence ∈ (0, 1]:

    σ = 0.0  →  confidence = 1.000  (perfect agreement, act immediately)
    σ = 0.5  →  confidence = 0.667
    σ = 1.0  →  confidence = 0.500  (moderate uncertainty)
    σ = 2.0  →  confidence = 0.333
    σ = 4.0  →  confidence = 0.200  (high uncertainty, do not act)
    σ = 9.0  →  confidence = 0.100

  The decision engine requires confidence >= 0.60 to act on a prediction.
  This corresponds to σ <= 0.667°C of ensemble disagreement. The threshold
  is intentionally permissive enough to act on moderate certainty while
  blocking action when the model is highly unsure.

  The function is monotone, differentiable, and has the correct asymptotic
  behaviour: it approaches 0 as σ → ∞ (total uncertainty → no confidence)
  and approaches 1 as σ → 0 (perfect certainty → maximum confidence). No
  other information is needed to justify its use beyond this.

STEP 4 — Combined temperature prediction

  pred_temp = cpu_pred × 0.6 + gpu_pred × 0.4

  CPU is weighted higher (0.6) because in the dataset's operational context
  (academic compute cluster), CPU load is the primary driver of chassis
  temperature. GPU load is significant but secondary. The 60/40 split reflects
  the empirical weight of CPU vs GPU in the thermal model used to generate
  training data (see simulator.py: load_avg = cpu×0.6 + gpu×0.4).

  combined_std = max(cpu_std, gpu_std)

  Conservative: we take the worst-case uncertainty across both models. If
  either model is uncertain, the combined confidence drops. This prevents
  overconfident decisions in the regime where one model is well-calibrated
  but the other is not.

STEP 5 — Velocity computation

  velocity = (pred_temp − current_temp) / PREDICTION_HORIZON

  Units: °C per minute.

  This is the estimated mean thermal slope over the next 30 minutes. It
  answers the question: "If this prediction is correct, how fast is this
  VM heating up on average?" A velocity of 0.5 °C/min means the VM will
  be 15°C hotter in 30 minutes. A velocity of 1.2 °C/min means it will
  be 36°C hotter.

  This is not the instantaneous derivative (which would require two consecutive
  predictions). It is the secant slope from current to predicted state. This
  is valid and preferable in a control context because it describes the average
  rate of approach to danger, which is what the scheduler and scaler need to
  know — not the momentary fluctuation.

  Velocity can be negative: the VM is predicted to cool down. The controller
  ignores negative-velocity VMs for scheduling/scaling purposes.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 6 — DECISION ENGINE: HOW VELOCITY AND CONFIDENCE DRIVE ACTIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

decision.py evaluates the entire fleet on every Kafka tick and returns exactly
one of three strings: "SCHEDULE", "SCALE_OUT", or "IDLE".

VM SCORING (used by both decision and scheduler)

  score = cpu × 0.3 + pred_temp × 0.4 + confidence × velocity × 10

  This composite score quantifies how "dangerous" a VM is as a task recipient:
    — cpu × 0.3:              current load contribution
    — pred_temp × 0.4:        predicted future heat (dominant term)
    — conf × velocity × 10:   confidence-weighted thermal trend

  The third term is critical: a VM with velocity = 2.0°C/min and confidence
  = 0.9 contributes 18 points to the score. The same velocity with confidence
  = 0.3 contributes only 6 points. This means the controller will not
  aggressively avoid a VM just because one uncertain tree predicted high
  temperature — the evidence must be consistent across the ensemble.

  Lower score = better candidate for receiving new tasks.
  Higher score = VM to move tasks away from.

AT-RISK CLASSIFICATION

  A VM is at-risk if ALL THREE conditions hold simultaneously:

  1. pred_temp > 80°C  OR  current temp > 80°C
       The prediction OR the observed value crosses threshold.
       Using OR means the system acts on either leading or concurrent evidence.

  2. confidence >= 0.60
       The prediction must be trustworthy. If σ is high (trees disagree),
       the at-risk classification is suppressed — the system waits for more
       consistent evidence rather than acting on noise.

  3. velocity >= 0.5°C/min
       The temperature must be rising. A VM at 82°C with velocity = −0.2
       (cooling down) should not trigger task migration — it is self-recovering.
       Only VMs that are both hot and still heating are classified at-risk.

SCHEDULE vs SCALE_OUT DECISION

  SCALE_OUT is chosen when:
    — at-risk fraction >= 0.5  (majority of fleet is endangered)
    — max_velocity across at-risk VMs >= 1.2°C/min (fast thermal rise)
    — no cool VMs exist (nowhere to redistribute tasks)

  SCHEDULE is chosen when:
    — at least one VM is at-risk
    — at least one cool VM exists (cpu < 85% AND pred_temp <= 80°C)

  IDLE is chosen when no VM meets the at-risk criteria.

  A 60-second cooldown in main.py (SCALE_COOLDOWN = 60) suppresses consecutive
  SCALE_OUT actions. After launching a new VM, the fleet needs time to absorb
  its metrics and re-evaluate. Without this guard, a single prolonged thermal
  event would trigger cascading scale-outs on every Kafka tick.

THE SCORE'S ROLE IN SCHEDULING

  After decision() returns "SCHEDULE", schedule() uses the score field that
  decision() wrote onto each VM dict. This avoids re-computing score in the
  scheduler — the decision pass already annotated every VM.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 7 — SCHEDULER: HOW TASK ASSIGNMENT WORKS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

schedule(tasks, vms) in controller/scheduler.py implements a greedy
best-fit assignment with priority ordering.

PRIORITY SORT

  Tasks are sorted by priority before assignment:
    high → normal → low    (mapped to 0, 1, 2)

  High-priority tasks get the first pick of available cool VMs. Low-priority
  tasks are assigned last and are most likely to be deferred if capacity
  runs out. This implements a basic preemptive-priority model without
  requiring preemption of running tasks — it operates at the boundary of
  queue consumption.

VM ELIGIBILITY FILTER

  Only VMs satisfying BOTH of the following receive tasks:
    pred_temp <= 80°C        (model does not predict overheating)
    cpu < 85%                (CPU headroom exists for the task's cpu_req)

  VMs that are hot but have the prediction trusted (at-risk VMs) are
  completely excluded. The scheduler never assigns to a VM the decision
  engine has identified as endangered.

FIRST-FIT ASSIGNMENT

  For each task:
    Iterate through eligible VMs sorted by score ascending (coolest first).
    Assign to the first VM where:
      current_cpu + task.cpu_req < CPU_CAPACITY_MAX (85%)

    On assignment:
      vm["cpu"]  += task["cpu_req"]
      vm["gpu"]  += task["gpu_req"] × 0.5   (GPU is shared, conservatively halved)

  The cpu field is updated in-memory immediately. This means subsequent
  tasks in the same scheduling round see the updated load — the scheduler
  is stateful within a round. This prevents the pathological case of all
  tasks being assigned to a single "coolest" VM simply because it had the
  lowest score at the start of the round.

UNASSIGNED TASKS

  Tasks that cannot fit in any eligible VM are returned in a list and placed
  back at the front of tasks[] in main.py. They will be re-attempted on the
  next scheduling tick (typically seconds later). This creates a natural
  backpressure mechanism: growing queue depth signals to operators (via the
  Grafana panel) that the fleet is undersized for the workload.

SECTION 8 — SCALER: LOCAL SIMULATION AND AWS EC2
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

scaler.py implements two paths, selectable via environment variable:

LOCAL PATH (_local_scale_out)

  Adds a new entry directly to the vms dict:
    {
      "vm_id":     "vm-N",
      "cpu":       15.0,
      "gpu":       10.0,
      "temp":      42.0,
      "prev_temp": 42.0,
    }

  The new VM starts at realistic idle values. On the next scheduling round,
  it will have the lowest score in the fleet and will absorb the most tasks.
  This is the intended behaviour — new VMs immediately become the primary
  targets for task redistribution.

AWS PATH (_aws_scale_out)

  Uses boto3.client("ec2").run_instances() with:
    — A pre-configured AMI (Amazon Linux 2, us-east-1)
    — Instance type t3.medium (2 vCPU, 4 GB RAM — adequate for the worker)
    — IAM instance profile tadr-ec2-role (requires ec2:RunInstances)
    — UserData bootstrap script that:
        yum install python3 git
        pip3 install kafka-python numpy
        git clone <repo>
        nohup python3 worker/producer.py --vm-id <new-vm-id> &

  The new VM is pre-registered in vms[] with idle values immediately upon
  boto3 call return (before the instance has booted). This prevents the
  60-second boot window from delaying the next scheduling round. Once the
  instance boots and the worker producer starts sending real metrics, the
  pre-registered idle values are overwritten by live data.

  scale_in() is also implemented: it selects the VM with the lowest cpu load
  and terminates it via ec2.terminate_instances(). This is called manually
  or could be triggered by a separate idle-detection policy.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 9 — KAFKA: WHY IT IS USED AS BOTH STREAM AND QUEUE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The system uses two Kafka topics with fundamentally different semantic roles:

vm-metrics (stream)
  Published by each worker every 5 seconds (EMIT_INTERVAL_SEC = 5).
  Consumer group offset is set to "latest" — the controller only cares about
  the most recent state of each VM, not historical messages. If the controller
  restarts, it picks up current state immediately without replaying hours of
  historical metrics.

  Each message overwrites the previous state for that vm_id in the vms dict.
  The topic behaves as a compacted state log.

task-queue (queue)
  Published by the task producer at variable intervals (2–5 seconds).
  Messages accumulate. The controller reads them and attempts assignment;
  unassigned tasks are returned to the in-memory tasks list (not re-published
  to Kafka). This is a deliberate simplification: in production, unassigned
  tasks should be re-committed to Kafka with an incremented retry count to
  survive controller restarts.

  Partitions: 3 for each topic.
  Replication factor: 1 (local demo; production should use 3).

The single consumer reading both topics (KafkaConsumer with two topic names)
uses a round-robin poll across partitions. This means the processing rate is
proportional to the combined arrival rate of both topics. Under heavy task
load with few VM updates, the controller will process mostly task messages;
under heavy VM churn the reverse occurs. The prediction and decision logic
runs on every consumed message regardless of topic — this ensures predictions
are refreshed on both task arrivals and metric updates.

SECTION 10 — MONITORING: PROMETHEUS AND GRAFANA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

exporter.py exposes eight gauge metrics at port 8000:

  tadr_vm_cpu_percent {vm_id}       — live CPU load per VM
  tadr_vm_gpu_percent {vm_id}       — live GPU load per VM
  tadr_vm_temperature {vm_id}       — current temperature per VM
  tadr_vm_pred_temperature {vm_id}  — predicted temperature at t+30 per VM
  tadr_vm_confidence {vm_id}        — ensemble confidence per VM
  tadr_vm_velocity {vm_id}          — thermal velocity in °C/min per VM
  tadr_task_queue_depth             — total pending tasks
  tadr_vm_count                     — total registered VMs

The pred_temperature gauge is the most operationally useful. Seeing the
predicted line (rendered as a dashed overlay in grafana.json) diverge upward
from the current temperature line gives operators 30 minutes of warning before
a thermal event occurs. This is the system working as intended.

The velocity panels include threshold annotations at 0.5 (schedule trigger)
and 1.2 (scale trigger). When the velocity line crosses these thresholds on
a VM with high confidence, the corresponding action in the controller loop
has already fired — but the Grafana panel confirms it visually.

The confidence panel shows the health of the prediction itself. Low confidence
on all VMs simultaneously indicates a regime the model has not seen before
(an unusual load pattern, hardware change, or ambient temperature outside
training distribution). This is an operational signal to review whether the
models need retraining.

SECTION 11 — END-TO-END FLOW: ONE COMPLETE TICK
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This describes exactly what happens from the moment a worker emits a metric
to the moment Prometheus is updated.

  T=0   VMSimulator.tick() runs on vm-2:
          burst event fires (1% probability hit)
          burst_cpu = 48.3, burst_ttl = 27
          raw_cpu = 62.1 (diurnal base) + 48.3 (burst) + gauss noise = 110.8
          → clamped to 99.0
          cpu  = prev_cpu × 0.7 + raw_cpu × 0.3 = 38.0 × 0.7 + 99.0 × 0.3 = 56.3
          temp = 45.2 + 0.15 × (25 + 56.3×0.45 + 0.326 - 45.2) = 47.9°C
          Emits: {vm_id: "vm-2", cpu: 56.3, gpu: 38.1, temp: 47.9}

  T=0+ε  KafkaProducer.send("vm-metrics", payload)

  T=~0   Controller consumer receives the message:
          topic = "vm-metrics"
          prev_temp = vms["vm-2"]["temp"] = 45.2   (stored from last tick)
          vms["vm-2"] updated with new data + prev_temp = 45.2

  T=~0   predictor.predict(vms["vm-2"]) called:
          X = [56.3, 38.1, 47.9, 45.2, 2.7, hour_sin, hour_cos]
          cpu_model: 100 trees predict → μ = 71.4, σ = 1.2
          gpu_model: 100 trees predict → μ = 64.8, σ = 0.9
          pred_temp  = 71.4 × 0.6 + 64.8 × 0.4 = 68.76°C
          combined_σ = max(1.2, 0.9) = 1.2
          confidence = 1 / (1 + 1.2) = 0.455
          velocity   = (68.76 - 47.9) / 30 = 0.695°C/min

          → confidence = 0.455 < CONFIDENCE_MIN (0.60)
          → vm-2 will NOT be classified as at-risk despite rising velocity
          → decision: IDLE (confidence gate suppressed action)

  T=~0   At the next tick, if load stays elevated:
          cpu = 72.4, temp = 54.1, prev_temp = 47.9
          trees now have more consistent evidence (the burst is sustained)
          σ drops to 0.6 → confidence = 0.625 > 0.60
          velocity = (81.3 - 54.1) / 30 = 0.907°C/min > VELOCITY_SCHEDULE
          pred_temp = 81.3 > CPU_TEMP_THRESHOLD
          → vm-2 is now at-risk
          → decision checks if cool VMs exist → vm-1 (cpu: 32%) is eligible
          → action = SCHEDULE

  T=~0   scheduler.schedule(tasks, vms):
          tasks sorted by priority: high-priority task t00042 (cpu_req=12) first
          eligible VMs: [vm-1 (score=28.4), vm-3 (score=34.1)]
          t00042 → vm-1: cpu 32% + 12 = 44% < 85% → assigned
          t00031 → vm-1: cpu 44% + 8  = 52% < 85% → assigned
          t00067 → vm-1: cpu 52% + 15 = 67% < 85% → assigned
          t00012 → vm-1: cpu 67% + 18 = 85% = 85% → NOT assigned (boundary)
          t00012 stays in remaining_tasks

  T=~0   update_metrics(vms, tasks):
          tadr_vm_cpu_percent{vm_id="vm-2"} = 72.4
          tadr_vm_pred_temperature{vm_id="vm-2"} = 81.3
          tadr_vm_confidence{vm_id="vm-2"} = 0.625
          tadr_vm_velocity{vm_id="vm-2"} = 0.907
          tadr_task_queue_depth = 1

  T=+10s Prometheus scrapes port 8000.
          Grafana renders updated panels.
          Operator sees vm-2 predicted line crossing 80°C threshold.
          Queue depth shows 1 pending task.

SECTION 12 — DESIGN DECISIONS AND THEIR JUSTIFICATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHY NOT PREDICT CPU LOAD AND DERIVE TEMPERATURE FROM IT

  An alternative design would predict CPU% 30 minutes ahead, then apply the
  thermal model to get predicted temperature. This two-stage approach has more
  failure modes: errors in load prediction compound with errors in the thermal
  model. Directly predicting temperature from a feature vector that includes
  current temperature (the single strongest predictor) is more accurate and
  simpler.

WHY RandomForest OVER LSTM

  LSTM is the common choice for time-series prediction and would likely achieve
  lower MAE on this dataset. However:
    — LSTM requires careful sequence length tuning and stateful management
      across the streaming consumer loop
    — LSTM does not naturally produce per-step ensemble variance for confidence
    — LSTM training is sensitive to learning rate and batch size; RandomForest
      is hyperparameter-robust
  For a system where confidence estimation is architecturally required, ensemble
  variance from RandomForest is a cleaner mechanism than MC Dropout or
  conformal prediction wrappers on an LSTM.

WHY VELOCITY IS (pred − current) / horizon AND NOT A RUNNING DERIVATIVE

  A running derivative (temp[t] − temp[t−1]) / Δt captures instantaneous
  noise. A brief 0.3°C measurement fluctuation produces a false spike in
  instantaneous velocity. The prediction-based velocity averages over the
  30-minute horizon, suppressing tick-level noise while capturing genuine
  thermal trends. It is also more directly actionable: "this VM will heat
  at 0.9°C/min on average over the next 30 minutes" is more useful than
  "this VM heated 0.3°C in the last 5 seconds."

WHY CONFIDENCE GATES ALL ACTIONS

  Without the confidence gate, a period of unusual load (a pattern the model
  saw rarely during training) would produce high-uncertainty predictions that
  happen to cross the temperature threshold. The controller would schedule or
  scale on unreliable evidence. The 0.60 confidence floor ensures that actions
  are taken only when the ensemble has seen enough similar patterns in training
  to make consistent predictions. When confidence is low on all VMs, the system
  degrades gracefully to IDLE rather than thrashing.

WHY THE SCORE FUNCTION WEIGHTS pred_temp AT 0.4

  The score is used to rank VMs as task recipients. pred_temp (coefficient 0.4)
  is the dominant term for hot-but-not-yet-at-threshold VMs. A VM with
  current cpu = 40% (low) but pred_temp = 79°C (nearly hot) should not receive
  tasks even though its current load is light. The pred_temp coefficient ensures
  that the scheduler looks ahead, not just at the current state.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 13 — LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1.  STATE IS IN-MEMORY ONLY
    The vms dict and tasks list are lost on controller restart. A Redis or
    DynamoDB state backend would provide durability. Unassigned tasks should
    also be re-published to Kafka rather than held in-memory.

2.  SINGLE KAFKA CONSUMER
    The controller runs single-threaded. Under high message volume (many VMs
    emitting at 5-second intervals), the consumer loop may fall behind. A
    partition-parallelised consumer group with separate prediction and decision
    threads would scale horizontally.

3.  MODELS ARE STATIC AFTER TRAINING
    The RandomForest models are trained once on 2021 data and serialised to
    .pkl files. Hardware refresh, workload shifts, or data centre relocation
    (different ambient temperature profile) would degrade prediction accuracy
    without retraining. An online learning wrapper (incremental RandomForest
    or a Hoeffding tree) would adapt to distribution shift automatically.

4.  CONFIDENCE IS HEURISTIC
    The 1/(1+σ) mapping is mathematically convenient but not calibrated.
    A calibrated confidence score would require post-hoc isotonic regression
    or Platt scaling against a held-out validation set, mapping raw σ values
    to empirical error probabilities. This would allow the confidence threshold
    (currently 0.60) to be interpreted as "act when the model is wrong less
    than 40% of the time" rather than as an uncalibrated heuristic cutoff.

5.  GPU TEMPERATURE THRESHOLD
    The GPU threshold is set to 82.5°C (mid-point of the 80–85°C design band).
    The current decision logic uses CPU_TEMP_THRESHOLD for both CPU and GPU
    at-risk classification. A separate GPU threshold check would allow finer
    GPU-specific scheduling when GPU temperature is the binding constraint.
